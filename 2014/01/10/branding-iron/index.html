Inexpensive Custom Branding Iron 10 Jan 2014

For a while now I've been wanting to be able to apply a craftsman signature to some of my pieces, but custom branding irons can be pretty expensive. Some people go the route of starting with a piece of flat steel and grinding away parts of it to reveal a pattern. Others bend metal into a simple pattern. For something like a signature, the first approach wouldn't produce as fine a result as I would like and the second approach isn't a real option.

Following an article on producing a 3D model of the brand and then buying a print of the part from shapeways. In the original article, the part was US$6, but mine weighed in at US$21.

It's helpful to be familiar with vector art, and ideally some basic 3D modeling since this approach involves a scanner and the following free software:

  • Inkscape
  • Blender
  • SketchUp

Inkscape

The process begins with getting a rough scan of a fancy signature. I played around with it on a sheet of paper about a dozen times with a sharpie until I found one that I liked. Once scanned, Inkscape can Trace Bitmap which generates a path. It's pretty noisy, so I simplified the path a couple of times to smooth it and then deleted all the stray speckles. The remaining control points needed a little tuning in places to get rid of some sharp points.

The brand will need a backing, so I created a second path that is in the rough shape of the outside of the design. Export the backing path in one file and the design itself in another in anticipation of importing them into Blender.

Blender

Unfortunately the free version of SketchUp doesn't allow you to extrude an arbitrary path, so importing these SVG paths into Blender is so we can take the flat paths and extrude them into 3D objects.

Extrude the design by 0.625mm (for a total thickness of 1.25mm) and the backing plate by 1.5mm (for a total thickness of 3mm, the minimum wall thickness for Shapeways when printing in stainless steel). Offset the backing plate to align it with the design. Remember to align it to the appropriate face so that the text will be in the right orientation when used as a branding iron.

Convert each of these into meshes and join them. Export it as a collada file.

SketchUp

The only reason to introduce SketchUp into the mix is so that we can scale the design to the exact dimensions. Do not scale the thickness, but only the height and width. While you're here, add a 1/4"-thick rod to the back so that it can be attached to a handle.

Shapeways

Finally, you're ready to use the model exported from SketchUp to upload it to Shapeways (or another service) and order a print.

Mounting and Using

I ended up boring out a hole in a steel rod and adding a set screw so that I can mount it. I anticipate making more of these and wanted to have the ability to interchange them.

For the handle, I turned a roughly palm-sized handle and epoxied the rod into it. When using it, I heat it up over an open flame and heat it up as evenly as I can and try it out a few times on a scrap piece of wood of the same species before applying it to the word piece.
